# Realistic and Interactive Robot Gaze

citation: M. K. Pan, S. Choi, J. Kennedy, K. McIntosh, D. C. Zamora,
G. Niemeyer, J. Kim, A. Wieland, and D. Christensen, “Realistic and
Interactive Robot Gaze,” in 2020 IEEE/RSJ Int. Conf. Intell. Robots Syst. (IROS), Oct. 2020, pp. 11 072-11 078, iSSN: 2153-0866.

## Abstract
Describes the development of a system for lifelike gaze in human-robot interactions using a humanoid Audio-Animatronics bust. Forms a general architecture to create gaze interactions that demonstrate the illusion of life.

## Introduction
To achieve human-realistic behaviours, animators must create fluid trajectories on the robot. There is the issue of the uncanny valley, so Disney animatronics have been limited to scripted shows rather than interaction. To produce authenticity, one must create the illusion of life.
This work draws upon principles used by animators to bring cartoon characters to life, including the use of arcs, overlapping action, drag, and slow in and slow out.

## Prior Work
Automated Methods for believable gaze:
•	Breazeal and Scassellati – mechanism relief on face, colour, and motion detection, with additional habituation input. The inputs were weighted by the motivations and emotions of their robot, ultimately resulting in a decision about whether to look at a person or a toy.
•	Zaraki – context-dependent social gaze-control system implemented as a part of a humanoid social robot. Use of proxemics, habituation, and attention modelling.
•	Vijayakumar – produced a saliency map from visual features. Problems with stabilizing the camera as the robot moves.

## Show
Show: Persona – elderly man reading a book, he has difficulty hearing and his eyesight is in decline. Constantly distracted from reading by people passing or greeting.

Three components: attention engine, behavior selection, and behavior library. Attention engine identifies lower-level salient stimuli in the environment. The system only examines stimuli generated by persons in the scene based on movements of the fitted skeletons.

### Attention Engine:
The attention engine generates a ‘curiosity score’ assigned to the person indicating their salience/significance as well as how important it is for the robot character to respond to them.
<p align="center">
  <img src="files\literature_images\gaze_curiousity_eqn.png" width="1000" alt="Curiousity Equation">
  <br>
  <em>Figure 2: Curiousity Equation.</em>
</p>
Θ is 0 or 1 and is a habituation factor, ϕ is curiosity, w is weighting, h indicates whether hands are raised above the shoulder. Can be tuned and expanded for character development (colors, sounds, etc...). The above function was created based on how people tend to attract others’ attention.
Also receives feedback from the behavior selection engine on which persons of interest have been attended to. If in gaze, the robot's curiosity about the figure decreases linearly. Habituation function limits the character from repeatedly responding to the same guest. Includes a mechanism to restore curiosity for a person after a period of time.

Θ(t+delta t) is 0 or 1 and is the habitation value at that time. Γ is equal to 1 when the robot is looking at the person and 0 otherwise. M hab is a negative habituation decay rate and m rest is a positive restoration rate.

### Behavior Selection Engine
Represents higher-level reasoning for the character–state machine that directs which behavioral state the robot is in. Maintains info about current state, curiosity thresholds, and state timeout durations.

States:
1.	Read: Default, reads a book located at torso level.
2.	Glance: A transition to Glance state from Read or Engage occurs when the attention engine indicates there is a stimulus with a curiosity score above a threshold. Below the threshold is Engage. The character gazes at the POI with eyes and varying degree of head motion away from the previous. Exits after timeout and returns to read. Or when another guest has high enough curiosity  to trigger engage.
3.	Engage: occurs when attention indicates there is stimuli with large enough curiosity to meet threshold and can be triggered from both read and glance. Gazes at person with both eyes and head. Exits when time in engage meets TO and when curiosity decreases below threshold.
4.	Acknowledge: triggered from Engage or Glace when POI is deemed familiar.

**Guestbase:**
Tracks people within the scene, short-term memory. Purpose:
1.	Store 3D points of a person (location of eyes and nose) to attempt mutual gaze
2.	Keep record for POI for purpose of habituation
3.	Denote special properties with relation to the system (familiar).
**Behavior Library:**
Show Objects: Consists of show animation definitions to drive motor actions and movements. Can be grouped (simultaneous), sequenced, looped (repeated for duration), and randomly selected. Can be added, scaled, and subtracted.
Behavior Layering: Organize behavior by subscribing to a subsumption architecture: a method of robot behavior and control which layers behaviors hierarchically – levels of competence. Lower = basic, higher = advanced processing. Higher levels are able to integrate, modify, suppress, or override lower.
1.	Zero Show: basic – default positioning
2.	Alive Show: minimum to be perceived as animate – breathing, blinking, saccades
3.	Read Show: Primary, the read state
4.	Glance Show: Glancing towards salient stimuli
5.	Engage Show: Character’s full attention being directed towards a stimuli, and subsumes both read and glance. 
6.	Acknowledge Show: Ran for familiar persons. Nodding action
**Implementation Lessons:**
1.	Saccades – helpful to have static camera. Requires carefully tuned controllers.
2.	Motion Bandwidth – overlapping action, some parts of character naturally move faster than others during motion. Eyelids at 10 Hz, Neck/head at 5 Hz.

## Discussion
Good from afar, good for short periods of time. At closer and longer durations – more complex behaviors are required. 
Uncanny valley, interaction is usually expected at such a close distance.
Tunable attention allows for reuse and freedom in character creation.
The subsumption architecture is created for mobile robots yet provides a good framework for organizing animatronic behaviors. Human psychomotive behavior can be intuitively modeled as layered behaviors with incoming sensory inputs, where higher behavioral levels are able to subsume lower behaviors.
Saccades increase realism – independent movement: eyes rotate towards each other to look at close objects and away for distant.

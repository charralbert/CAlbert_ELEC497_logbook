# The Relationship Between Perceived Agency and Trust in Robots

Citation: Frazier, C., Malcolm McCurry, J., Zish, K. et al. The Relationship Between Perceived Agency and Trust in Robots. Int J of Soc Robotics 17, 1563–1578 (2025). https://doi.org/10.1007/s12369-025-01285-8

## Abstract
The competency and integrity of a robot will impact how a human will trust it. Perceived agency is also an important determinant of how much people will trust a robot. Experiment 1 showed that people trusted a robot that followed social norms more than one that violated them/did not follow. Robots that have more agency are trusted, even when they violate social norms. 

## Introduction
Competency-related trust typically relates to how capable, reliable, and predictable a robot is while performing tasks and is frequently associated with robot-as-tool. Integrity related trust typically relates to interpersonal or moral trust based on how dependable, sincere, moral, and reliable an agent is in a social relationship. Integrity is associated with robot-as-teammate.
The impact of competency on trust is stronger than the impact of integrity on trust.
The more intelligent a robot is, the more people trust it. The more anthropomorphic a robot is, the more people seem to trust it. The more autonomous a robot is, the more trustworthy. Autonomy can be considered both a positive and a negative competency. These non-standard features fall into agency. People perceive agency in another entity when their actions may be assumed by an outside observer to be driven by its internal thoughts and feelings and less by the external environment.
Agency is described as how much an entity can plan and act or the amount an entity can plan, think, communicate, etc.

### Social Norms as a method to Study Perceptions of Robots 
A social norm is an expectation about appropriate behavior that occurs within a group context. Social norms are usually formed in groups about both individuals and groups. Some are taught, but usually its observed. Social norms are especially useful and relevant in situations where the individual can observe current behavior and merge it with their understanding to "fit in."
Several studies show that people think that robots default behavior should be to follow the social norms in the context of groups of humans.
When robots follow norms they have higher perceived agency rather than those that don't (relatively). If a robot queues in line it has higher perceived agency than one that cuts the line. However, if a robot cheats (a perceived social norm violation) it increases perceived agency. This may be because cheating may cause people to believe the robot has strong self-interest.
Little empirical research on the relationship between social norms and trust, people do trust robots more when they act in an expected manner.

### Attitutes Towards Robots Impacting Trust
Prior attitudes towards robot can negatively impact judgement.
Three hypotheses:
1. Examine social norms-trust hypothesis: Robots that follow social norms will be trusted more than robots that violate social norms.
2. Examine the social norms-perceived agency hypothesis: Robots that conform to social norms will be perceived as having more agency than robots that violate social norms. 
3. Propose the new perceived agency-trust hypothesis: robots that have more perceived agency will be trusted more than robots that have less perceived agency; thus perceived agency is an additional dimension upon which people decide to trust a robot. 

## Norming Study
3 conditions: norm violating condition, norm conforming condition, mistake condition and 3 different scenarios: line (queue/cut), trash(trash can/litter), elevator(standing distance). 
Removed mistake condition because participants found it difficult to differentiate between mistakes and norm violations. 

## Experiement Results
Participants felt a greater sense of trust for a norm conforming robot compared to a norm violating robot.
Norm-conforming robot rated higher perceived agency than norm-violating.
Negative attitudes towards robot did not impact measure.
